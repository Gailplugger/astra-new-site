# Email Spoofing & Phishing: How Hackers Trick You (And How to Spot Fakes)

## Why This Matters To You

Every day, millions of people fall victim to phishing attacks. In India, email-based cybercrimes have increased by 45% in the last two years alone. And the scary part? **Hackers are getting better at tricking us.**

You might think, "I won't fall for it," but these attacks are designed by professionals who study human psychology. They know exactly how to make a fake email look legitimate.

---

## What Is Email Spoofing?

**Email spoofing** is when someone sends an email pretending to be from someone else. It's like forging a letter with someone else's signature—except on the internet, it's surprisingly easy to do.

Here's how it works:

- A hacker gets access to your email server or uses a tool that can manipulate the "From" field
- They send an email that **looks exactly** like it came from your bank, your school, or even your friend
- Most email clients display only the sender's name, not the full technical details
- You get fooled into clicking a malicious link or downloading a dangerous file

---

## Phishing Attacks: The Real Threat

**Phishing** is the criminal use of spoofing. It's a targeted attack designed to steal your personal information, passwords, or money.

### Common Phishing Scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Fake Bank Alert**
```
From: security@yourbank.com
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Suspicious Activity Detected
Body: "Your account has been locked due to suspicious activity. Click here to verify your identity immediately or your account will be closed."
```

The link takes you to a fake website that **looks exactly** like your real bank. You enter your username, password, and 2FA code—and boom, the hacker owns your account.

**Scenario 2: Job Offer Scam**
```
From: hr@famouscompany.com
Subject: Job Offer - Rs 50,000/month
Body: "Congratulations! You've been selected. Download the offer letter and complete the KYC form using this link."
```

The "offer letter" is actually malware or a fake form that steals your Aadhar, PAN, or bank details.

**Scenario 3: Instagram/Facebook "Verification"**
```
From: support@instagram.com
Subject: Verify Your Account
Body: "Your account may be locked. Verify here: [fake-instagram.com]"
```

You log in to the fake site, and your real Instagram is now hacked.

## Red Flags: How to Spot Fake Emails

### 1. **Check the Sender's Email Address**

This is the #1 mistake people make. They read "security@bank.com" and trust it.

**Reality check:** Hover over the sender's name to see the actual email address.
- ✅ Real: `security@sbi.co.in`
- ❌ Fake: `security@sbi-update.com` or `security.update@gmail.com`

### 2. **Look for Generic Greetings**

Legitimate companies use your real name.
- ❌ "Dear User" or "Dear Customer"
- ✅ "Dear Kartik"

### 3. **Urgency & Threats**

Phishers create panic to rush you into action:
- "Account locked in 24 hours!"
- "Verify NOW or lose access!"
- "Click immediately!"

**Real companies don't pressure you like this.** They give you time.

### 4. **Suspicious Links**

Before clicking any link:
- Right-click and copy the link address
- Check if the domain matches the sender
- ❌ Link says "Verify" but points to `verify-now-secure.ru`
- ✅ Bank link goes to the official bank domain

### 5. **Grammar & Spelling Mistakes**

Professional companies proofread their emails.
- "Click her to confirm"
- "Plesae verify your account"
- Multiple typos = automatic red flag

### 6. **Attachments from Unexpected Senders**

- "Project report_URGENT.exe" — **Never download .exe, .zip, or .bat files** from unknown sources
- If you weren't expecting an attachment, don't open it

### 7. **Asking for Passwords or OTP**

**No legitimate company will ever ask you to share:**
- Your password
- OTP or 2FA codes
- Credit card numbers
- Aadhar or PAN details

## Real Case Studies: How These Attacks Work in India

### Case 1: The SBI Account Takeover (2023)

A teenager received an email titled "SBI Account Security Alert" asking him to verify his account. The email looked identical to official SBI messages.

He clicked the link and entered:
- Username
- Password
- Login PIN
- OTP

Within 10 minutes, ₹2,50,000 was transferred from his account.

**What he missed:** The sender's email was `security-update@sbi-india.site` (not the real `.co.in` domain)

### Case 2: Job Scam (2024)

A girl from Delhi got a job offer from "Amazon HR" with a salary of ₹60,000/month. She downloaded the "offer letter" which contained malware.

The malware:
- Captured her bank details
- Installed a keylogger to record her passwords
- Gained access to her email and linked social accounts

**What she missed:** Real job offers come through official company websites and LinkedIn, not random emails

### Case 3: Instagram Account Hijacking

A common attack: fake "Instagram verification" emails claiming the account will be deleted. The link goes to a fake Instagram login page.

Thousands of Indian teens have lost their accounts this way.

## How to Protect Yourself: Action Plan

### ✅ Step 1: Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Even if someone gets your password, they can't log in without your phone.

**How:** Go to your email/social media → Security Settings → 2FA → Use an authenticator app (not SMS)

### ✅ Step 2: Use a Password Manager

Remember unique, strong passwords for each account.

**Recommendation:** Bitwarden (free) or 1Password

### ✅ Step 3: Enable Email Alerts

Let your bank/email provider alert you of login attempts from new locations.

### ✅ Step 4: Verify Links Before Clicking

Hover over links. Check the actual URL destination.

### ✅ Step 5: Report Phishing Emails

- Gmail: Click "Report phishing"
- Outlook: Click "Junk" → "Phishing"
- Official companies often have a reporting email (e.g., `fraud@bank.com`)

### ✅ Step 6: Keep Your Devices Updated

Security patches fix vulnerabilities. Don't skip updates.

## What If You've Already Clicked a Phishing Link?

Don't panic. Here's what to do:

1. **Don't enter any information** if you realize it's fake
2. **Change your password immediately** (from a different device)
3. **Alert your bank** if it involves finances
4. **Enable fraud alerts** on your accounts
5. **Check your credit report** for unauthorized activity
6. **Run an antivirus scan** on your device
